Joseon Dynasty (1392–1897)

The Joseon Dynasty, founded by Yi Seong-gye (King Taejo) in 1392, ruled Korea for over 500 years, making it one of the longest dynasties in East Asian history. It shaped Korea’s identity through Confucian values, scholarly governance, and major cultural achievements that still influence the Korean Peninsula today.

Government and Society

Joseon established a highly organized and centralized government led by the king. The ruling class, known as the yangban, consisted of scholar-officials who studied Confucian texts and served in government posts. Entry into the bureaucracy was determined through the civil service examinations, which tested knowledge of Confucian philosophy.

Society was structured into clear classes: yangban (nobles), commoners, and slaves (nobi). Confucian ideals shaped daily life, emphasizing family loyalty, education, morality, and social order.

Culture and Science

The Joseon era produced major cultural and intellectual achievements. One of the most significant contributions was the creation of Hangul, the Korean alphabet, invented in 1443 by King Sejong the Great to improve literacy among common people.

Advances in science, astronomy, agriculture, and medicine flourished. The dynasty also saw the development of traditional Korean ceramics, landscape painting, and classical literature.

Buddhism and Confucianism

Although Buddhism had been influential in previous dynasties, Neo-Confucianism became the dominant ideology in Joseon. It shaped government laws, education, family structure, and social values.

Buddhism was restricted but survived through mountain temples, monastic communities, and religious art. These temples became centers of spiritual life outside major cities.

Defense and Foreign Relations

Joseon faced several external threats, including the Japanese invasions (1592–1598), known as the Imjin War. Korean naval commander Admiral Yi Sun-sin famously defended the peninsula using innovative turtle ships.

In the 17th century, Joseon endured invasions by the Manchus, but despite these conflicts, the dynasty continued to evolve culturally and politically.

Legacy

The Joseon Dynasty profoundly shaped Korean culture, language, and social structure. Its palaces—such as Gyeongbokgung and Changdeokgung—remain iconic symbols of Korean history. Confucian traditions established during Joseon continued to influence Korea into the modern era.
