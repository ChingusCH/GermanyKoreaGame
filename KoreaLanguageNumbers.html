Numbers & Counting in Korean

Korean uses two numbering systems: Sino-Korean and Native Korean.

Korea uses two different numbering systems: Sino-Korean numbers, derived from Chinese, and Native Korean numbers, which are unique to Korea. Each system is used in different contexts.

1. Sino-Korean Numbers (일, 이, 삼…)

Sino-Korean numbers are used for dates, money, time units, and formal numbering.

Used for:

• Dates (years, months, days)
• Money
• Addresses
• Phone numbers
• Counting minutes or seconds

Numbers 1–10:

일 (il)
이 (i)
삼 (sam)
사 (sa)
오 (o)
육 (yuk)
칠 (chil)
팔 (pal)
구 (gu)
십 (sip)

Example:

2025년 11월 18일 → 이천이십오년 십일월 십팔일

2. Native Korean Numbers (하나, 둘, 셋…)

Native Korean numbers are used for counting people, objects, age, hours, and floors.

Used for:

• Counting people or objects
• Age
• Hours (telling time)
• Floors in buildings

Numbers 1–10:

하나 (hana)
둘 (dul)
셋 (set)
넷 (net)
다섯 (daseot)
여섯 (yeoseot)
일곱 (ilgop)
여덟 (yeodeol)
아홉 (ahop)
열 (yeol)

Examples:

세 사람 (3 people)
다섯 개 (5 items)
네 시 (4 o’clock)

Counting Objects

Counters in Korean specify what kind of object is being counted.

Korean uses special counters after numbers:

개 (gae) → general objects
명 (myeong) → people
장 (jang) → flat objects (paper, tickets)
마리 (mari) → animals

Examples:

사과 세 개 → three apples
학생 다섯 명 → five students

Cultural Notes

Korean age traditions differ from the international system.

Korean numbers are closely tied to daily life and tradition. Historically, Koreans added one year at birth and another on New Year’s Day. Today, most people use the international age system, but the traditional system is still culturally recognized.
