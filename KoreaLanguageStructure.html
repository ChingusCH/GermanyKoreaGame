Korean Sentence Structure

Korean follows a Subject–Object–Verb structure, with verbs appearing at the end.

Korean has a sentence structure that is different from English, which can take some practice for learners. The basic word order in Korean is Subject–Object–Verb (SOV), meaning the verb usually comes at the end of the sentence.

Basic Word Order

Subject (S) – The person or thing doing the action.
Object (O) – The thing receiving the action.
Verb (V) – The action, placed at the end.

Example:

English: I eat rice. (S–V–O)
Korean: 저는 밥을 먹어요. (Jeoneun bap-eul meogeoyo)

저는 = I (subject)
밥을 = rice (object)
먹어요 = eat (verb)

Particles

Particles in Korean mark grammatical roles such as subject, object, and topic.

Particles are small markers attached to nouns that show the noun’s role in the sentence.

Subject particles: 이 / 가
Object particles: 을 / 를
Topic particle: 은 / 는

Example:

친구가 사과를 먹어요. (Chingu-ga sagwa-reul meogeoyo)

친구가 = the friend (subject)
사과를 = apple (object)
먹어요 = eats

Additional Notes

• Adjectives come before the noun they describe.
Example: 예쁜 꽃 (pretty flower)

• Time and place appear before the verb.
Example: 저는 어제 학교에 갔어요. (I went to school yesterday.)

• Politeness levels change verb endings depending on formality and relationship.

Cultural Significance

Politeness levels in Korean reflect respect, hierarchy, and social context.

Korean sentence structure reflects a high-context culture where respect and hierarchy matter. Verb endings and particles communicate politeness, social standing, and subtle meaning differences.
