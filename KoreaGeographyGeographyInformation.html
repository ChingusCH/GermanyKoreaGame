South Korea: Geography Overview

South Korea occupies the southern half of the Korean Peninsula in East Asia. It is bordered by the Yellow Sea to the west, the Sea of Japan (East Sea) to the east, and the Korea Strait to the south. Its only land border is with North Korea along the Demilitarized Zone (DMZ), one of the most heavily fortified borders in the world.

The country is largely mountainous, especially in the east where the Taebaek Mountains run parallel to the coastline. As a result, most major cities and farmland lie in the western and southern lowlands. The highest peak is Hallasan, a dormant volcano on Jeju Island, rising 1,947 meters above sea level.

Several rivers shape the nation's geography. The Han River flows through Seoul and has long served as a vital transportation and economic artery. Other major rivers include the Nakdong and Geum.

South Korea has a temperate climate with four distinct seasons: cold winters, warm springs, hot monsoon summers, and cool autumns. Most rainfall occurs in summer, often during short and intense monsoon periods. The country also experiences late-summer typhoons.

Although relatively small, South Korea contains diverse ecosystems—from coastal wetlands to alpine forests. Jeju Island’s volcanic landscape, including lava tubes, is so unique that it is recognized as a UNESCO World Heritage Site.
