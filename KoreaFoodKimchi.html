Kimchi Varieties

A selection of kimchi showcasing Korea’s diverse fermented dishes.

Kimchi is Korea’s most iconic fermented dish, made primarily from vegetables, seasonings, and sometimes seafood. It is a staple in Korean cuisine and accompanies almost every meal. While baechu kimchi (napa cabbage kimchi) is the most famous, there are hundreds of regional and seasonal varieties.

Common Types of Kimchi

Baechu, Kkakdugi, Oi Sobagi, Dongchimi, Chonggak, and Bossam kimchi are popular examples.

Baechu Kimchi (Napa Cabbage Kimchi)
The classic kimchi made from napa cabbage, radish, garlic, chili powder, and fish sauce. Often served as a side dish or used in soups and stews.

Kkakdugi (Cubed Radish Kimchi)
Made with cubed Korean radish and similar seasonings to baechu kimchi. Crunchy texture and slightly sweet flavor.

Oi Sobagi (Cucumber Kimchi)
Made by stuffing cucumbers with a spicy mixture of garlic, chili, and green onions. Popular in summer for its refreshing taste.

Dongchimi (Water Kimchi)
A mild, watery kimchi made with radish, water, and minimal seasoning. Often eaten cold as a refreshing side dish or soup.

Chonggak Kimchi (Ponytail Radish Kimchi)
Made from small whole radishes with leafy tops. Known for its crunchy texture and strong flavor.

Bossam Kimchi
A richer, wrapped kimchi often filled with various vegetables, seafood, and sometimes chestnuts. Served on special occasions or in high-end meals.

Cultural Significance

Kimjang, the traditional kimchi-making season, reflects Korean seasonal and regional diversity.

Kimchi reflects Korea’s seasonal and regional diversity. Families traditionally make large batches during Kimjang, the kimchi-making season in late autumn, to last through winter. The fermentation process enriches kimchi with probiotics, vitamins, and minerals, making it both nutritious and flavorful.
