Cultural Do’s and Don’ts in South Korea

Understanding etiquette helps build respectful and positive relationships in Korea.

Korean culture places strong importance on respect, hierarchy, and social harmony. Knowing basic etiquette helps avoid misunderstandings and shows appreciation for local customs.

Cultural Do’s

A polite bow is the foundation of respectful interaction.

  • Bowing: Bow slightly when greeting elders, teachers, or in formal settings. A deeper bow shows greater respect.
  • Use Two Hands: Offer or receive items (gifts, money, drinks) with both hands — a sign of politeness.
  • Remove Shoes Indoors: Always take off your shoes in homes, certain restaurants, traditional stays, and temples.
  • Address People Properly: Use titles and last names for someone older or higher in status.
    Example: “Kim 선생님” (Teacher Kim).
  • Respect Elders: Let them sit, start eating, enter a room, or speak first.
  • Try Korean Food Politely: At least taste dishes served to you; leaving food untouched may seem rude.

Cultural Don’ts

Some actions considered normal elsewhere can be impolite in Korea.

  • Do Not Point at People: Pointing with a finger is rude; gesture with your whole hand instead.
  • Do Not Tip: Tipping is uncommon and sometimes confusing; service staff do not expect it.
  • Do Not Raise Your Voice: Public arguments or displays of anger are frowned upon.
  • Do Not Blow Your Nose at the Table: Seen as impolite and unpleasant during meals.
  • Do Not Disrespect Elders: Avoid interrupting, contradicting, or ignoring older people.
  • Do Not Touch Someone’s Head: The head is considered the most sacred part of the body.
