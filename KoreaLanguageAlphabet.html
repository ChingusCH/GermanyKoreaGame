Hangul Alphabet

Hangul, the Korean alphabet, is logical, phonetic, and easy to learn.

Hangul is the Korean alphabet, created in 1443 by King Sejong the Great during the Joseon Dynasty. Before Hangul, Koreans primarily used Chinese characters (Hanja) for writing, which were difficult for common people to learn. Hangul was designed to be simple, logical, and easy to learn, allowing literacy to spread widely.

Structure of Hangul

Hangul combines consonants and vowels into syllable blocks for easy reading.

Hangul consists of 14 consonants and 10 vowels in its basic form, which combine into syllable blocks. Each syllable block contains at least one consonant and one vowel.

Consonants: ㄱ (g/k), ㄴ (n), ㄷ (d/t), ㄹ (r/l), ㅁ (m), ㅂ (b/p), ㅅ (s), ㅇ (silent/ng), ㅈ (j), ㅊ (ch), ㅋ (k), ㅌ (t), ㅍ (p), ㅎ (h)
Vowels: ㅏ (a), ㅑ (ya), ㅓ (eo), ㅕ (yeo), ㅗ (o), ㅛ (yo), ㅜ (u), ㅠ (yu), ㅡ (eu), ㅣ (i)

For example, the word 한국 (Hanguk, “Korea”) is written as:
ㅎ (h) + ㅏ (a) + ㄴ (n) = 한 (Han)
ㄱ (g/k) + ㅜ (u) + ㄱ (k) = 국 (Guk)

Features of Hangul

Phonetic: Each letter represents a specific sound.
Syllable blocks: Letters are grouped into blocks for readability.
Scientific design: Consonants reflect the shape of the mouth and tongue when pronouncing them.
Ease of learning: Hangul can be mastered in a few days, unlike Chinese characters.

Cultural Significance

Hangul Day (October 9) celebrates the creation and importance of the Korean alphabet.

Hangul is a source of pride for Koreans and is celebrated annually on Hangul Day (October 9). Its creation revolutionized literacy in Korea and remains one of the most efficient and logical writing systems in the world.
